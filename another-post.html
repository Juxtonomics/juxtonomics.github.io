The Price of Bread and the Weight of Empires

Sid McDowell August 4, 2025

A single loaf in 1789 could cost a day's wages. But what truly broke was not the baker’s scale—it was the system itself.

Introduction

In every upheaval, there is a grain of hunger. Before flags are raised or monarchs fall, it is the marketplace that whispers first. Juxtonomics is concerned not with the dramatic—but the gradual.

Paris, 1789

A four-pound loaf of bread cost 15 sous.
A skilled worker made 20.
A family needed two loaves.
Math does the rest.

And when the numbers no longer worked, neither did the people.

Economic Fractures Beneath Political Myths

Historians often describe revolutions as matters of ideology. But economic records tell us something simpler: debt, inflation, and subsistence collapse systems faster than slogans ever could.

  • Grain shortages preceded political speeches.

  • Coin debasement outpaced royal reform.

  • Bread riots were not chaos—they were clarity.

A Juxtonomic Question

What breaks first: the economy or belief in it?

When the same pattern appears in 1790, 1929, 2008, and again in whispers today, we don’t need prophecy. We need precedent.

Closing Thought

Economic history is not dead data. It is a pulse. And if we listen close enough, we can hear it racing again.

Juxtonomics is the art of reading between receipts. And revolution, as always, begins in the kitchen.
